Every bowl of ramen is built from four parts: the tare (seasoning base), the broth, the
noodles, and the toppings. The "style" of a bowl is mostly a statement about the first
two, and once you can read them, an unfamiliar menu stops being intimidating.

## The four seasoning families

- **Shoyu (soy sauce):** the oldest and most common style, clear brown broth with a sharp,
  savory edge. Tokyo's default bowl.
- **Shio (salt):** the lightest tare, a pale, clean broth that lets the stock speak.
- **Miso:** fermented soybean paste, rich and nutty. Born in Sapporo; see
  [regional ramen across Japan](/blog/regional-ramen-across-japan/) for the story.
- **Tonkotsu (pork bone):** technically a broth, not a tare: bones boiled until the
  collagen emulsifies into a creamy white soup. Compared in depth in
  [tonkotsu vs shoyu](/blog/tonkotsu-vs-shoyu-broth-explained/).

## Broth versus tare

A common confusion: tonkotsu names the _broth_, while shoyu, shio, and miso name the
_tare_. That is why "tonkotsu shoyu" is not a contradiction: it is a pork-bone broth
seasoned with a soy-sauce base. Most bowls you will meet are a combination like this.
