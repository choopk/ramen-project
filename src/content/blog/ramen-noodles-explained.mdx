Ramen noodles are wheat noodles made with kansui, an alkaline salt solution that gives them
their characteristic yellow color, firm bite, and distinct flavor. The word _ramen_ itself
does not describe a shape or thickness — it describes a noodle made with kansui. Japanese
food law actually requires noodles to contain at least 0.01% kansui to be labeled ramen.
Everything else — thickness, curl, hydration — is a variable that shops adjust to match
their broth.

## What kansui actually does

Kansui (鹹水) is a solution of alkaline salts, typically potassium carbonate and sodium
carbonate dissolved in water. It has been used in Chinese noodle-making for centuries, and
it is the single ingredient that separates ramen from every other wheat noodle on the
planet.

Three things happen when kansui hits wheat flour:

**Color.** Flour contains flavonoid pigments that are colorless at neutral pH but turn
yellow in alkaline conditions. This is why ramen noodles are yellow and pasta is white —
it is chemistry, not food dye. The more kansui, the more intense the yellow.

**Texture.** Kansui strengthens the gluten network in wheat dough. The alkaline salts
cause proteins to cross-link more aggressively, producing noodles that are firmer, more
elastic, and more resistant to breaking. This is why ramen noodles have a springy bite
that pasta does not — the kansui has physically restructured the protein matrix.

**Flavor.** Alkaline dough has a distinct taste — slightly mineral, faintly soapy in
isolation, but in context it reads as "ramen." This flavor is what makes ramen taste like
ramen even before the broth enters the picture. Remove the kansui and you have a plain
wheat noodle that would dissolve into mush in hot broth.

The ratio of potassium carbonate to sodium carbonate matters. Potassium carbonate produces
a harder, snappier noodle with a stronger yellow color. Sodium carbonate produces a
softer, chewier noodle with a milder color. Most commercial kansui is a blend, and noodle
makers adjust the ratio depending on what they need: more potassium for thin noodles that
need to hold up to rich broth, more sodium for thick noodles that should be chewy rather
than crunchy.

## Thickness and shape

Noodle thickness is measured in millimeters, and it changes the eating experience more
than almost any other variable:

**Thin (1.0–1.25 mm).** Fast cooking, delicate texture, absorbs broth quickly. Used in
Hakata tonkotsu and light shio ramen. The noodle is designed to be eaten fast — _kaedama_
(extra noodle servings) exists because thin noodles go soft in hot broth within minutes.

**Medium (1.5–2.0 mm).** The middle ground. Versatile enough for shoyu, shio, and lighter
miso broths. This is the most common noodle thickness in Japanese ramen shops.

**Thick (2.5–3.0 mm).** Chewy, substantial, slow to soften. Built for heavy broths —
rich miso, tonkotsu, and tsukemen dipping broth. A thick noodle can sit in a hot bowl
longer without turning to mush.

**Ultrathick (3.5+ mm).** Jiro-style and specialty shops. Almost pasta-like in presence.
Meant to be chewed, not slurped.

Shape matters as much as thickness:

**Straight noodles** slurp easily and move fast from bowl to mouth. They carry less broth
per strand because their smooth surface does not trap liquid. Used in Hakata tonkotsu and
light, clean broths where the soup should not overwhelm the noodle.

**Wavy noodles** have ridges and curves that trap broth in their folds. Each slurp
delivers more soup with the noodle. This is why shoyu and miso shops tend to use wavy
cuts — the broth clings to the noodle, and the eating experience is more integrated.

**Flat noodles** (like Kitakata's signature cut) have a wider surface area that holds
broth differently. They feel more substantial in the mouth and tend to be used in lighter
broths where the noodle itself is a feature, not just a vehicle.

The cross-section matters too. Round noodles feel smoother and blend into the broth.
Square noodles have sharper edges that create a more defined bite and a different
mouthfeel. Most ramen noodles are round, but square cuts exist at specialty shops.

## Hydration level

Hydration refers to the ratio of water to flour in the noodle dough — and it has a
direct effect on texture:

**Low hydration (28–33%).** Firm, snappy, absorbs broth aggressively. The noodle has less
water in the dough, so when it hits hot broth, it drinks more of the soup. This is why
Hakata-style tonkotsu noodles are low-hydration — they are designed to absorb the rich
pork broth quickly, delivering flavor with each bite.

**Medium hydration (33–38%).** The versatile middle. Springy but not too absorbent. Works
with most broth styles. This is where most shoyu and shio noodles sit.

**High hydration (38–45%).** Fluffier, chewier, cleaner mouthfeel. The dough already has
more water, so it absorbs less broth during eating. Used in Sapporo miso ramen (where the
thick, rich broth would overwhelm a more absorbent noodle) and in tsukemen (where noodles
are served cold and need to maintain their texture without hot broth to soften them).

The relationship is simple: low-hydration noodles drink more broth; high-hydration noodles
drink less. Match the hydration to the broth's richness and you get a balanced bowl. Mismatch
them and either the noodle drowns in soup or the broth sits untouched in the bowl.

## How noodles and broth pair

The fundamental rule: as broth richness increases, noodle thickness, firmness, and
alkalinity should increase too. A thin, delicate noodle disappears in heavy tonkotsu. A
thick, chewy noodle overwhelms light shio.

Here is how the major styles pair:

**Hakata tonkotsu:** Ultra-thin, straight, low-hydration, firm. Designed to be eaten
quickly (_kaedama_ system) and to absorb the rich pork broth without falling apart.

**Sapporo miso:** Thick, curly, high-hydration, yellow. The curl traps the thick miso
broth, and the high hydration prevents the noodle from becoming soggy in the heavy soup.

**Tokyo shoyu:** Medium-thickness, wavy, moderate hydration. The wave holds the clear
broth, and the moderate chew balances the soy-seasoned stock.

**Hakodate shio:** Thin to medium, straight, moderate hydration. Light enough to let the
delicate broth speak, but with enough body to not feel insubstantial.

**Kitakata shoyu:** Thick, flat, wavy, high-hydration. The unusual shape is the point —
these noodles are chewy, substantial, and designed to be the star of a relatively light
broth.

The pairing is not arbitrary. Every element of a noodle — its thickness, shape, hydration,
kansui ratio — is a response to the broth it will serve. A ramen shop that serves great
broth but the wrong noodle is like a great singer with a bad microphone. The sound does
not reach you properly.

## The noodle-making process

Making ramen noodles is simpler than making pasta, but the kansui changes everything:

1. **Mix.** Flour, kansui solution, salt water, and sometimes egg are combined into a
   stiff, dry dough. Ramen dough is much drier than pasta dough — it looks crumbly at
   first and requires mechanical force to bring together.

2. **Knead.** The dough is passed through rollers repeatedly, folding and compressing.
   This develops the gluten network that kansui has strengthened. The dough becomes
   smoother and more elastic with each pass.

3. **Age.** The dough rests — sometimes for hours, sometimes overnight. Aging lets the
   kansui fully react with the flour proteins and allows the gluten network to relax
   slightly. This is when the characteristic flavor develops.

4. **Cut.** The aged dough is rolled to the target thickness and cut to the target shape.
   Thin straight, thick wavy, flat — the cutter determines the final form.

5. **Portion.** Noodles are weighed and portioned for individual bowls. Fresh ramen
   noodles are sold in portions of 120–150 grams.

Most ramen shops buy noodles from specialist makers rather than making them in-house.
Noodle-making is its own craft — shops like Sun Noodle in the United States and various
regional makers in Japan supply thousands of restaurants. The relationship between a ramen
shop and its noodle maker is often long-standing and collaborative, with the maker
adjusting thickness, hydration, and kansui ratio to match the shop's specific broth.

## Fresh vs. dried vs. instant

Three forms of ramen noodles exist, and they are not interchangeable:

**Fresh noodles** (生麺, _nama-men_) are the standard at quality ramen shops. They have
high moisture content, cook in 30–90 seconds, and have the best texture. They degrade
quickly — most should be used within a week of production.

**Dried noodles** (乾麺, _kan-men_) have had most of their moisture removed. They cook in
3–5 minutes, last months in the pantry, and have a firmer, slightly chewier texture. Used
at some shops and common for home cooking.

**Instant noodles** (インスタント麺) are flash-fried after cooking, which removes moisture
and creates the porous texture that allows them to rehydrate in boiling water in 2–3
minutes. Invented by Momofuku Ando in 1958, instant ramen is a different product entirely —
convenient and consistent, but not the same as fresh or dried noodles at a shop.

The distinction matters when evaluating ramen. A shop serving fresh, properly made noodles
is operating at a different level than one using dried or, worse, instant noodles. The
texture difference is immediately obvious.

## Bottom line

Ramen noodles are defined by kansui, and everything else is a response to the broth they
will serve. Thickness, shape, hydration, and kansui ratio are all tuned to create a
specific eating experience. When you understand these variables, you can read a noodle
shop's menu with your eyes: thin and straight means light broth, thick and curly means
heavy broth, and the color tells you how much kansui is in the dough.
