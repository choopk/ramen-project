Ramen is intensely local. Nearly every region of Japan has a bowl it claims as its own,
shaped by climate, local ingredients, and the shops that defined it. This stub sketches
the three most famous examples; the full article will map many more.

## Sapporo: miso ramen

Hokkaido winters produced Japan's heartiest bowl: a rich miso broth, often finished with
butter and corn, with thick, chewy yellow noodles that stand up to it. Miso is the
youngest of the [main ramen style families](/blog/guide-to-japanese-ramen-styles/),
invented in Sapporo in the 1950s.

## Hakata: tonkotsu ramen

Fukuoka's yatai (street stall) culture built the tonkotsu standard: a milky pork-bone
broth over ultra-thin noodles, with kaedama (noodle refills) so the noodles never sit
long enough to soften. The broth itself is broken down in
[tonkotsu vs shoyu](/blog/tonkotsu-vs-shoyu-broth-explained/).

## Kitakata: shoyu ramen

A small city in Fukushima with one of the highest ramen-shop densities in Japan. Flat,
wide, curly noodles in a light shoyu broth — and locals famously eat it for breakfast
(asa-ra).

## Bottom line

Regional style is the best lens for planning a ramen trip: pick the bowls first, then the
cities. More regional deep-dives will follow this stub.
