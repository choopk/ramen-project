Shoyu ramen is a Japanese noodle dish built on a soy-sauce seasoning base, called
_tare_, combined with a clear chicken, pork, or seafood stock. It is the oldest of the
four major ramen families and the most widely served style across eastern Japan. If you
walk into a ramen shop in Tokyo without knowing what to order, you will almost certainly
get shoyu.

## What shoyu ramen actually is

The word _shoyu_ (醤油) simply means soy sauce in Japanese. That is the starting point:
a concentrated soy-sauce tare is ladled into the bowl first, then hot broth is poured over
it, and noodles are added on top. The result is a clear, amber-colored soup with a sharp,
savory edge and a depth that comes from fermentation, not from fat or collagen.

This is the key distinction from other ramen families. Tonkotsu is defined by its broth.
Miso is defined by its paste. But shoyu is defined by its _tare_, the seasoning layer
that sits between the stock and the finished bowl. That is why two shoyu ramen shops can
use completely different broths (one chicken-based, one pork-and-seafood) and both still
count as shoyu. The soy sauce tare is the constant.

A typical shoyu tare is not just straight soy sauce from a bottle. Most shops blend
several ingredients: soy sauce as the base, mirin for sweetness, sake for aroma, and often
dried seafood, mushrooms, or kombu for additional umami. The exact recipe is usually
secret. The tare is combined with broth at roughly a 1:10 ratio, a few tablespoons of
concentrated seasoning into a full bowl of stock.

## How the broth is made

Shoyu ramen broth is usually a _chintan_ (clear soup), not a _paitan_ (cloudy, emulsified
soup). The most common base is chicken bones simmered gently with aromatics. Some shops
add pork bones, dried sardines (_niboshi_), kombu seaweed, or dried shiitake mushrooms.
The goal is a clean, transparent stock that carries flavor without heaviness.

The simmering matters. A hard boil turns broth cloudy and greasy. Shoyu ramen broth is
cooked at a gentle bubble, just enough to extract flavor from bones and aromatics over
several hours, but not so aggressive that fat and collagen emulsify into the liquid. The
finished broth should be clear enough to see the noodles at the bottom of the bowl.

This is where shoyu sits on the spectrum. Compared to shio ramen (which uses an even
lighter broth), shoyu has more body from the soy sauce. Compared to tonkotsu (which is
thick enough to coat a spoon), shoyu is delicate and drinkable. It is the middle ground,
flavorful enough to satisfy, light enough to finish the bowl.

## Noodles that match

Shoyu ramen noodles are typically medium-thickness, wavy, and moderately hydrated. They
need enough chew to stand up to the broth without drowning in it, but not so thick that
they overpower the delicate stock.

The shape matters as much as the thickness. Wavy noodles trap more broth in their curves,
so each slurp carries more of the soy-seasoned soup. This is different from the thin,
straight noodles you find in Hakata tonkotsu, which are designed to cook fast and absorb
less broth. Shoyu noodles are built to marry with the soup, not fight it.

Most shoyu ramen shops use noodles with moderate kansui (alkaline salt) content. The
kansui gives ramen its characteristic yellow color and firm bite, but shoyu noodles are
not pushed to the extreme firmness you find in tonkotsu shops. A good shoyu noodle has a
springy, slightly soft center that softens the line between noodle and broth.

## The standard toppings

A classic shoyu ramen bowl has a predictable set of toppings, predictable because they
work, not because shops are lazy:

- **Chashu**: braised pork belly, sliced thin. The fat melts into the broth as you eat.
- **Menma**: fermented bamboo shoots. Crunchy, slightly tangy, a textural counterpoint to
  soft noodles.
- **Narutomaki**: the white-and-pink fish cake with a spiral pattern. Mild flavor, mostly
  visual.
- **Nori**: dried seaweed sheets. Umami-rich, and they soften as they absorb broth.
- **Negi**: chopped scallions. Fresh, sharp, cuts through the richness.
- **Ajitama**: marinated soft-boiled egg. Not always standard, but increasingly common.

The toppings are deliberately restrained. Shoyu ramen is about the broth and noodle
relationship, not about piling on extras. A bowl with too many toppings would overwhelm
the clarity that defines the style.

## Where shoyu ramen came from

The story starts in 1910, in Tokyo's Asakusa district. A restaurant called Rairaiken
(來々軒) opened under the ownership of Ozaki Kan'ichi, a former customs official. The
shop served Chinese-style noodle soup, but Ozaki made one critical change: he replaced
the Chinese salt tare with Japanese soy sauce.

That substitution is the birth of shoyu ramen as we know it. Soy sauce carries its own
layered umami from fermented soybeans, and it behaves differently in broth than plain
salt, it adds color, depth, and a distinctly Japanese character. Rairaiken served around
2,500 customers daily, and its shoyu-based noodle soup became the template that Tokyo
ramen shops still follow.

Before Rairaiken, noodle soup in Japan was called _shina soba_ ("Chinese soba"). After
World War II, the term shifted to _ramen_, borrowed from the Chinese _lāmiàn_ (pulled
noodles), even though Japanese ramen is rolled and cut, not pulled. The name stuck, and
shoyu remained the default style in Tokyo through the postwar ramen boom of the 1950s
and 60s.

The 1980s _ramen boom_ elevated shoyu from everyday fast food to something approaching
culinary art. Television specials, guidebooks, and a culture of queuing at famous shops
turned ramen into a national obsession. Shoyu, as the oldest and most familiar style,
became the baseline against which all other styles were measured.

## How shoyu compares to the other families

|                     | Shoyu                | Shio                | Miso                | Tonkotsu             |
| ------------------- | -------------------- | ------------------- | ------------------- | -------------------- |
| **Defined by**      | Tare (soy sauce)     | Tare (salt)         | Tare (miso paste)   | Broth (pork bone)    |
| **Broth clarity**   | Clear amber          | Clear pale          | Opaque, thick       | Opaque, creamy       |
| **Flavor profile**  | Savory, sharp, umami | Light, clean, salty | Rich, nutty, hearty | Heavy, fatty, porky  |
| **Typical noodles** | Medium, wavy         | Thin, straight      | Thick, curly        | Ultra-thin, straight |
| **Home turf**       | Tokyo, eastern Japan | Hakodate            | Sapporo             | Hakata, Kyushu       |

The fastest way to read a ramen menu: shoyu broth is brown and clear, shio is pale and
clear, miso is thick and cloudy, tonkotsu is white and creamy. Learn these four visual
cues and you can order with confidence anywhere in Japan.

## How to spot good shoyu ramen

A few things separate a great shoyu bowl from a forgettable one:

**Broth transparency.** If you cannot see the noodles through the broth, the stock was
boiled too hard. Good shoyu broth is clear enough to read the bottom of the bowl through
it.

**Tare balance.** The soy sauce should enhance the broth, not dominate it. If the first
thing you taste is straight soy sauce, the tare-to-broth ratio is off. You should taste
the stock first, then the soy seasoning layer underneath.

**Noodle texture.** The noodles should have visible waves that hold broth. Flat, straight
noodles in shoyu ramen are a red flag: they suggest the shop is using the wrong cut for
the style.

**Finishing oil.** Many shoyu shops add a thin layer of flavored oil on top: sesame oil,
chicken fat, or sometimes _mayu_ (burned garlic oil). This keeps the broth hot and adds
aroma. If the surface is completely dry, the bowl is missing a layer.

## Variations on the theme

Shoyu ramen is not a single recipe. It is a family of styles that share a soy-sauce tare
but differ in broth, noodles, and regional character:

- **Tokyo classic shoyu**: the Rairaiken template. Chicken-and-sardine broth, medium
  wavy noodles, restrained toppings. The archetype.
- **Tanrei shoyu**: a modern, lighter variant. Clearer broth, less oily, often with a
  dashi-forward approach. Popularized by shops like Rokurinsha and endorsed by the
  Michelin Guide.
- **Kitakata shoyu**: from Fukushima. Light soy broth with unusually thick, flat, wavy
  noodles. Locals eat it for breakfast (_asa-ra_).
- **Tokushima shoyu**: dark, sweet, almost syrupy broth with thin curly noodles. Often
  served with a raw egg cracked on top. A regional oddity.
- **Iekei**: Yokohama's hybrid. Pork-bone-and-chicken broth with a soy tare, thick
  noodles, large nori sheets, and spinach. Not pure shoyu, but soy-driven.

Each of these is a distinct enough sub-style to warrant its own deep-dive. The point is
that "shoyu ramen" is a broad category: the real diversity lives in the regional and
shop-level variations underneath it.

## Bottom line

Shoyu ramen is the ramen you should learn first. It is the oldest style, the most common
in eastern Japan, and the one that best demonstrates the tare-and-broth relationship that
defines all ramen. Learn to read a shoyu bowl: the clear broth, the wavy noodles, the
restrained toppings, and you have a framework for understanding everything else.
