Tonkotsu and shoyu are the two bowls most travelers meet first, and they could hardly be
more different: one is an opaque, creamy pork-bone soup, the other a clear, sharp,
soy-seasoned classic. This is a stub comparison; the full article will cover technique,
history, and where to try the defining examples of each.

## Tonkotsu: the emulsified broth

Pork bones boiled hard for many hours break down until fat and collagen emulsify into the
broth itself. The result is milky, heavy, and coats the thin, low-hydration Hakata-style
noodles it is usually served with. Kyushu (Hakata in particular) is its home turf, as
covered in [regional ramen across Japan](/blog/regional-ramen-across-japan/).

## Shoyu: the seasoned classic

Shoyu ramen is defined by its tare rather than its stock: a soy-sauce seasoning base cut
into a (usually) clear chicken or pork stock. It is the oldest of the styles in the
[main ramen style families](/blog/guide-to-japanese-ramen-styles/) and remains the
default bowl across much of eastern Japan.

## How to tell them apart

Look at the broth before you taste it. Opaque white-beige and creamy: tonkotsu. Clear
brown with visible oil droplets: shoyu. The noodle cut is a second tell: ultra-thin and
straight for Hakata tonkotsu, wavier and medium for a Tokyo shoyu.

## Bottom line

Broth opacity is the fastest read on what is in front of you. Learn these two poles and
every bowl in between makes more sense.
